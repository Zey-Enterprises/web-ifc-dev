Usually, yes. Diet soda is not a health food, but it can be a useful adherence tool if it replaces sugar-sweetened beverages and does not lead you to eat more elsewhere. From a body-composition standpoint, the important comparison is the whole pattern: a zero-calorie drink that helps you stay within {% include glossary.html id="calorie-balance" text="calorie balance" %} is often better than a regular soda that adds calories you did not plan.

Safety depends on the specific sweetener and amount, but approved sweeteners have safety reviews and acceptable intake limits.{% include citation.html id="fda-2025-aspartame-and-other-sweeteners-food" label="1" %} If diet soda worsens cravings, reflux, bloating, headaches, or sleep because of caffeine timing, use less or switch products. If it helps you stay consistent while the rest of the diet is nutrient-dense, it can stay.
