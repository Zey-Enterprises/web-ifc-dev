For generally healthy adults, approved {% include glossary.html id="nonnutritive-sweeteners" text="nonnutritive sweeteners" %} are considered safe under their permitted conditions of use by the FDA, which continues to monitor the evidence and sets acceptable daily intake levels for approved food-additive sweeteners.{% include citation.html id="fda-2025-aspartame-and-other-sweeteners-food" label="1" %} The practical question is usually not whether a normal amount of diet soda or sugar-free yogurt is toxic. It is whether that product helps your overall diet run better.

They can be useful when they replace sugar calories without triggering compensatory eating. Controlled trials summarized in a meta-analysis found modest improvements in body-weight outcomes when low-calorie sweeteners replaced regular-calorie alternatives.{% include citation.html id="miller-2014-low-calorie-sweeteners-body-weight-composition" label="2" %} They are not mandatory, and some people dislike the taste or notice cravings, headaches, or gastrointestinal symptoms with specific products.

The best rule is simple: use them as a tool, not as the foundation of the diet. If they help you reduce added sugar while still eating mostly protein, produce, fiber-rich carbohydrates, and appropriate fats, they are usually reasonable.
