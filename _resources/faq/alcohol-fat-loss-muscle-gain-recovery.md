{% include glossary.html id="alcohol" text="Alcohol" %} can fit into a diet, but it is not a neutral input. It provides calories, lowers inhibition, can displace protein or nutrient-dense food, and often makes the surrounding food decisions harder. For fat loss, the largest issue is usually not that alcohol has a magical fat-storage pathway. It is that it adds energy, is metabolized preferentially, and often makes the rest of the evening less consistent with {% include glossary.html id="calorie-balance" text="calorie balance" %}.{% include citation.html id="breslow-2020-alcohol-dietary-intake" label="1" %}

For muscle gain and recovery, the concern is dose. Occasional low intake is unlikely to erase a good training block, but heavier intake can impair sleep, hydration, muscle protein signaling, next-day training quality, and food execution.{% include citation.html id="barnes-2014-alcohol" label="2" %} If body composition is the priority, keep alcohol planned, bounded, and separated from your hardest training and lowest-calorie days when possible.

Practical guardrails: decide the number of drinks before the first one, use low-calorie mixers, alternate with water, eat a real protein-containing meal first, and avoid turning the drink decision into an unplanned food environment decision.
