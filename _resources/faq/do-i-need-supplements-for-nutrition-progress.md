No. Supplements can be useful, but they sit on top of the basics: energy intake, protein, food quality, training, sleep, and consistency. A supplement cannot compensate for a diet that routinely misses its main targets.

The useful supplement category is narrow: things that solve a real problem. Protein powder can make protein easier. {% include glossary.html id="creatine" text="Creatine" %} can support high-intensity training. {% include glossary.html id="caffeine" text="Caffeine" %} can improve alertness or performance when timed well. A multivitamin may be reasonable insurance when food variety is temporarily constrained, but it does not replace produce, protein, fiber, or an appropriate calorie target.

For athletes subject to drug testing, supplement risk is not just physiology; it is contamination and labeling. Use third-party-tested products and check sport-specific rules before assuming a supplement is allowed.
