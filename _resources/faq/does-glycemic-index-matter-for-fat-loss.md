{% include glossary.html id="glycemic-index" text="Glycemic index" %} can be useful context, but it is not a primary fat-loss rule. A food's glycemic index is measured when that food is eaten alone, while real meals often include protein, fat, fiber, and mixed ingredients that change digestion and blood-glucose response.

For fat loss, calories, protein, food volume, fiber, and repeatability usually matter more. Low-glycemic foods like beans, lentils, oats, fruit, and intact grains can be excellent because they are often filling and nutrient-dense. High-glycemic foods are not automatically bad; white rice, potatoes, cereal, or sports drinks may fit well around training or when digestion needs to be easy.

Use glycemic index as a secondary clue about food behavior, not as a moral ranking system.
