Dietary fat supports hormone production, cell membranes, fat-soluble vitamin absorption, food enjoyment, and calorie adequacy. It is also energy-dense, so it can either help a muscle-gain phase or quietly make a fat-loss phase harder.

The quality of fat matters too. A practical default is to get most fats from sources such as olive oil, nuts, seeds, avocado, eggs, fatty fish, and other minimally processed foods, while moderating {% include glossary.html id="saturated-fat" text="saturated fat" %} and minimizing {% include glossary.html id="trans-fat" text="trans fat" %}.{% include citation.html id="de-souza-2015-intake-of-saturated-and-trans-unsaturated-fatty-acids-and-risk-of-all-cause-mortality-cardiovascular-disease-and-type-2-diabetes" label="1" %}

Very low-fat dieting can become hard to sustain and may reduce food satisfaction. Very high-fat dieting can crowd out carbs and fiber. The right amount depends on calories, preferences, training, and health markers.
